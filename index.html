Analyzing Kurt Lewin's leadership categories, it was interesting to realize how Japanese practice of "Nemawashi" is used in Japanese companies to naturally balance and cycle through Autocratic, Democratic and Laissez-Faire leadership. Nemawashi is a Japanese term for collaborative and democratic decision making, where common consensus and compliance is negotiated between followers and stakeholders before management and leaders will actually review or being notified of the problem or issue. Japanese workers argue, discuss and seek consensus quetly and secretly, thus, when problem or issue is elevated to the respective leaders and managers, they automatically agree and sign up for whatever workers came up with democratically with full consensus. This is a sign of respect from leaders to followers, and decision they made is not influenced by leaders (laissez-faire), unquestioned by leaders when presented by workers (democratic) and unquestioned by workers again, after it is accepted as action plan (autocratic), even enforced by management via highly transactional and task-oriented approach.

Latter Half: Week 7 to 12

Week 7 Reflection

When it comes to reflections, the most meaningful and insightful ways for me and the teams I had pleasure to work with was Hansei - a method widely used in Japan, but most actively promoted by Toyota, as part of TPS and Toyota Kata.


Hansei is a unique, cyclic and mandatory regular reflection framework, and the power of its' philosophy lies in the fact, that each person reflect irregardless of project or work beind done, or not done - failed or sucessfull. We reflect on what was done, why was it done this specific way, if we had to do it again - what would we do differently, and what small incremental improvements can we make to make the work marginally, but sensibly better.


The unique spice of the lower management in Japan is that it wraps Transactional Leadership and Alienated (critical, but passive) Followership into productive mix and find a beauty in both via unique perspective.


Hansei, combined with Alienated Followership means that people are capable of conveying interesting, critical and analytical reflection on the work, while also being passive and compliant executors when management commands to do something. Alienated workforce can make rich contributions to the way Management learns to work, learns THE work and learns perspectives on improvements and change that comes from bottom to the top, while being able to effectively and rapidly enforce new knowledge or positive change onto highly compliant people.

Week 8 Transactional and Transformational Leadership

Kicking the good old Japs agains, their Hoshin Kanri framework is an intricate illustration on how Japanese companies can lead effectively even in large organizations by utilizing different leadership styles on different levels of management.


On the lectures and workshops we learned that leadership can be excessively directive and almost directionless, going from Transactional, through Transformational, to Laissez-Faire leadership, which is very close philosophycally to the Hoshin Kanri diagram, in which top management sets up a general, long term and vague aspirations of what company should be like, what it should strive for and what is the purpose of work and company's existence's justification.


Japanese Middle Management is engaged in the Kaizen - tiny incremental changes using PDCA framework - which is the great example of the Transformational leadership, where visionaries with lightest hands transcend their vision onto people directly responsible with setting goals and keeping them realistic and achieveable, while striking for more.


Lower grade of Japanese leadership is highly Transactional, as it is tasked to carefully and diligently handle daily routines and operations with stable and even quality and reliability of the operations, hence being very strict, not really permissive and sometimes overtly controlling.

Week 9 Authentic Leadership & Inclusive Leadership

During the workshop on the Inclusive Leadership, we were asked to debate and reason either agreement or disagreement with the statement, which was pretty heated and innately divisive - on whether true inclusion achievable only with the structural reforms.


Opinion I had to defend - and the one I strongly support - is that without collectivism, social harmonization and unified standardization of culture, no people can effectively support each other towards the common goal or problem. The great examples of this is Hostile Compliance widely spread in Fascist Italy against Mussolini Regime, and in Poland during German Occupation.


In both cases, population would fully comply with all the laws and reforms on paper, hence structure formally being established and ran. However, both italians and poles would show extreme levels of personal and communicational hostility and obstruction, and would refuse to do anything without a very specific paper making a very specific order or command to do so, effectively ruining any productivity or collaboration to a meaningless levels.


Humanity is not ruled by papers, it is ruled by Humans.


Another question was on whether meritocracy and inclusion truly coexist in the workplace, and my personal believe is that meritocracy in most cases is mere delusion, and the only way both can co-exist is if management shows absolute beast of a performance in micromanaging talents and hidden potential of each employee, be it a DEI diversity hire or a generic worker. A lot of people posses hobbies, aspirations, interests or long-forgotten skills that might benefit company in some way, but actually activating these skills, elevating them to appropriate level and utilizing them in financially and economically sound manner seems to be an extremely unbearable level of overhead for the management personelle in absolute majority of the cases.


Most people are cognitively simple, and would not be able to utilize such intricacies effectively on operational level.

Week 10 Change Leadership & Blue Ocean Leadership

Both Japan and Israel are developed and prosperous non-germanic liberal democracies with high standards of healthcare, wellfare, public infrastructure, institutions and education. However, both countries despite having extremely concerning and underwhelming geography, tackle the youth education in practically opposite ways.


While Israelis teach to never believe anything, question everything and endure a hardcore reasoning and debates on every occasion, to the point where teachers purposefully lie, Japanese people teach collective effort, compliance, submission, respect, order and rigid hierarchy.


What I truly believe is an innovative leadership is an ability to assess whether specific fields and operations require precision and discipline, or creativity and boldness, and assemble teams and lead in either Japanese or Israeli way.


Creating social and collective harmony is hard and Conficianist countries like Japan engraved very specific frameworks and instruments to do so, like Hoshin Kanri charts, where middle and upper management can align their actions properly.


Israeli schools and secret services promote qualities like "Bitzua", "Rosh Gadol", "Davka" - being a big head, being bold, smug and smut, confident, willing to take hard issues out of spite, and take responsibility when opportunity is visible.

Week 11 Responsible and Sustainable Leadership

Considering the way corporate environment and modern market economies are structured, Responsible and Sustainable leadership feels like something hardly achievable. Jack Welch, one of the best performing CEOs of the North America and author of many theories and books about long term strategic management had proven himself that the system is juxtaposed to mumble about sustainable future while financially forcing or incentivizing the corporations and shareholders to "collect pennies in front of the train", maximizing risk for tiniest, most meaningless gains in margins.


I think international conglomerates pushing agenda about profit-hungry machines being "moral" or responsible is just a mind-play to reduce regulations - as if companies can make their one remotely sane and responsible decisions and take everyone into account without governmental oversight, which I have to point out as insane, even as hardcore laissez-faire libertarian.


The only remotely sane movement or social theory that might combine operationally-sound market economy and long-term sustainability is the degrowth and de-urbanization, but this is not something companies, shareholders or overall economic agents would voluntarily participate in. As when corporations grow aggressively, they induce demand for product, push harder on market research, invest in R&D, hire more people and generally drive economy forward - degrowth would gradually debilitate this process, giving everyone dismorale for all of its' components except the slow down of planet's resource depletion.
